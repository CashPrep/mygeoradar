## Introduction: Why This Matters More Than Any SEO You've Done Before

Something fundamental changed in how people find businesses. It didn't happen with a press release. It happened quietly, across millions of daily conversations.

People started asking AI instead of Googling.

Not for everything — but for the decisions that matter most to your business. "Who's the best accountant near me?" "What's the best project management tool for a 10-person team?" "Which roofing company in Denver actually has good reviews?" "What software should I use for email marketing?"

These used to go to Google. A significant and growing share of them now go to ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Claude.

Here's what makes this different from every other marketing channel you've had to learn:

**AI assistants don't show a list. They give an answer. One confident, named answer.**

If your business is in that answer, you get the customer with almost no friction — they came pre-sold, pre-informed, already trusting the recommendation. If you're not in that answer, you don't get a "second place" click. You simply don't exist in that moment.

This playbook is your complete system for making sure you're in the answer.

## Part 1: How AI Assistants Actually Decide What to Recommend

### The short version

AI assistants are trained on massive amounts of text from the internet. When you ask one to recommend a business, it draws on what it learned during training — plus, for some assistants, real-time web search. It synthesizes all of that into a confident-sounding response.

The businesses that get recommended are the ones that AI has "heard of" from the most credible, consistent, and detailed sources.

That's the entire game. Your job is to be heard of — clearly, consistently, and credibly — across every source AI pays attention to.

### The four trust signals AI looks for

**1. Entity clarity**
Can AI unambiguously identify your business? Does it know your exact name, what you do, where you are, who you serve, and how to distinguish you from similarly named businesses? If there's any ambiguity — if "Smith & Co." could be three different businesses — AI won't confidently recommend you.

**2. Cross-source consistency**
AI cross-references. It looks for your business name, address, phone number, and description appearing the same way across multiple independent sources. If your name is "ABC Plumbing" on your website but "ABC Plumbing Services LLC" on Yelp and "A.B.C. Plumbing" on Google — that inconsistency signals unreliability.

**3. Third-party credibility signals**
AI weights mentions from sources it considers authoritative. That includes local news, industry publications, major review platforms, .edu and .gov references, and high-authority directories. A business mentioned in the Austin Business Journal carries more weight than one mentioned only on its own website.

**4. Structured, machine-readable information**
AI models are very good at reading structured data. Schema markup (covered in detail in Part 3) tells AI crawlers *exactly* what your business is in a format they can ingest with zero ambiguity. Businesses with complete schema markup give AI no reason to guess.

### Why ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Claude behave differently

These four assistants use different underlying architectures and data sources, which means the same business can be invisible on one and well-known on another.

**ChatGPT (OpenAI)**
Primarily draws from its training data. With web browsing enabled, it also pulls from live search. Strong Google Business Profile data, Wikipedia/Wikidata presence, and mentions in high-authority publications carry the most weight. ChatGPT is particularly good at recognizing "entities" it has seen mentioned in multiple contexts.

**Perplexity**
This is the most "live search" of the four. Perplexity runs actual web searches for most queries and synthesizes the results. This means recent reviews, recent press mentions, and fresh website content matter here more than anywhere else. If your website is thin or outdated, Perplexity shows it.

**Google Gemini**
Deep integration with Google's own data. Your Google Business Profile, Google Maps presence, Google reviews, and structured data that Google's crawlers have indexed all have an outsized effect here. If you've maximized your Google presence, Gemini is likely your strongest assistant.

**Claude (Anthropic)**
Claude tends to be more conservative — it'll say "I'm not sure I have reliable information on that specific business" more readily than ChatGPT will. To get Claude to mention you confidently, you need to have been written about in sources Claude considers authoritative (major publications, established industry sites). Claude is the hardest to crack but the most valuable when you do — it signals strong third-party credibility.

## Part 2: Your Foundation — The 5 Things That Must Be Perfect

Before any advanced tactics work, these five foundations must be airtight. Don't skip ahead.

### Foundation 1: Google Business Profile (GBP) — Complete, Accurate, Active

Your Google Business Profile is the single most leveraged asset you have for AI visibility. Google's Knowledge Graph feeds ChatGPT (via Bing/web), Gemini directly, and Perplexity's search results. A complete GBP is the baseline for all other AI visibility work.

**Do this — in this exact order:**

1. Go to [business.google.com](https://business.google.com) and claim your listing if you haven't.
2. Verify ownership (postcard, phone, or video verification).
3. Fill in your **primary category** with precision. Don't pick "Restaurant" if you're a "Seafood Restaurant." The more specific, the better.
4. Add **3–5 secondary categories** that describe every service you offer.
5. Write a **business description** of at least 250 words. Use complete sentences. Mention your services, your location, what makes you different, and who you serve. AI assistants literally read this description.
6. Upload a minimum of **10 photos**: exterior, interior, team, products/services, and a logo.
7. Add **every service and product** you offer with descriptions and prices if applicable.
8. Set your **hours** including special hours for holidays.
9. Add your **website URL** — make sure it matches exactly what's on your website (www vs. non-www, http vs. https).
10. Enable **messaging** and respond to every message within 24 hours.
11. Post to your GBP at least **once per week** — an update, an offer, an event, or a photo. Active profiles rank higher and are referenced more by AI.

**The review strategy:**
Ask every satisfied customer for a review. Use this exact script:

> *"Hey [Name], really glad we could help. If you have 60 seconds, a Google review would mean a lot to us — it helps other [describe customer type] find us. Here's the direct link: [your GBP review link]"*

AI models count review volume and average rating. 50 reviews averaging 4.8 stars is dramatically more powerful than 10 reviews at 5.0.

**Responding to reviews:**
Respond to every review — positive and negative — within 48 hours. Use your business name and mention your service in your response. This creates keyword-rich, crawlable content directly on your GBP.

### Foundation 2: NAP Consistency — Name, Address, Phone

NAP consistency is the bedrock of local AI visibility. If your business information appears differently across sources, AI treats it as noise rather than signal.

**Your NAP must be identical everywhere:**
- Full legal business name (decide: "Smith Plumbing" or "Smith Plumbing LLC" — pick one, use it everywhere)
- Complete address (decide: "123 Main St" or "123 Main Street" — pick one)
- Phone number in the same format (decide: "(512) 555-1234" or "512-555-1234" — pick one)

**Audit your current NAP:**
1. Google your business name and look at every result
2. Check Yelp, Bing Places, Apple Maps, Facebook, LinkedIn, BBB, Chamber of Commerce
3. Document every variation you find
4. Update each one to match your canonical NAP

This is tedious. Do it anyway. NAP inconsistency is one of the top reasons businesses don't get recommended by AI despite having a good Google presence.

### Foundation 3: Your Website's Foundational Pages

Your website is the source AI trusts most that you fully control. Three pages in particular are read by AI crawlers more than any others.

**Your Homepage**
Within the first 200 words of your homepage, the following must be unambiguously clear:
- Your exact business name
- What you do (service/product)
- Where you do it (location or service area)
- Who you serve
- How to contact you

If a first-time AI crawler reads your homepage and can't answer those four questions instantly, you have a problem.

**Your About Page — the most underused AI asset you have**
Most About pages say "We were founded in 2015 with a passion for excellence." This is useless to AI.

Rewrite your About page using this formula:

*[Business name] is a [type of business] in [location] that helps [specific customer type] with [specific problem]. Founded in [year] by [founder name], we have [credibility signal: years of experience / number of clients / certifications]. We specialize in [top 3 services]. Our clients typically come to us because [unique differentiator]. [Business name] is [any awards, certifications, associations].*

This paragraph alone, if written well, is something AI assistants can quote almost verbatim in a recommendation. Make it quotable.

**Your /facts or /about page addition — your AI knowledge base**
Create a dedicated section (or separate page) titled something like "About [Business Name]" or "[Business Name] — Key Facts" and include:

- Year founded
- Founder name(s)
- Number of employees
- Services offered (bulleted list)
- Service area / locations
- Certifications and licenses
- Awards and recognitions
- Associations and memberships
- Notable clients or projects (if appropriate)
- Press mentions with links

This page exists purely to give AI a structured, dense, factual reference. You're essentially writing AI's answer for it.

### Foundation 4: Bing Webmaster Tools

Most people ignore Bing. Big mistake. Microsoft Copilot — which is built into Windows, Edge, Microsoft 365, and used by hundreds of millions of people — is powered by Bing's index. If Bing hasn't indexed your site well, you're invisible to Copilot.

**Do this:**
1. Go to [bing.com/webmasters](https://bing.com/webmasters) and sign in with a Microsoft account
2. Add your website
3. Verify ownership (HTML file, meta tag, or DNS record)
4. Submit your sitemap (it's at yourdomain.com/sitemap.xml for most sites)
5. Use the URL Submission tool to submit your 5 most important pages directly
6. Check the IndexNow settings — enable it for automatic submission of new content

This takes 20 minutes and has a direct, measurable impact on your Copilot visibility.

### Foundation 5: Your SSL, Speed, and Crawlability

AI assistants won't recommend businesses with broken, slow, or insecure websites. This sounds basic — it is basic — but it's frequently the hidden reason businesses underperform.

**Check these right now:**
1. **SSL:** Your site must load on HTTPS. If it loads on HTTP, your host can add a free SSL certificate via Let's Encrypt in minutes.
2. **Speed:** Use [pagespeed.web.dev](https://pagespeed.web.dev). Your mobile score should be above 70. If it's below 50, contact your developer — slow sites are crawled less frequently and weighted lower.
3. **robots.txt:** Go to yourdomain.com/robots.txt — make sure you're not accidentally blocking AI crawlers. It should allow all or explicitly allow Googlebot, Bingbot, and GPTBot.
4. **Sitemap:** Go to yourdomain.com/sitemap.xml — it should exist and list all your important pages. Submit it to Google Search Console and Bing Webmaster Tools.

## Part 3: Schema Markup — The Language AI Speaks

Schema markup is the single highest-leverage technical action you can take for AI visibility. It's invisible to users, but it's how AI crawlers read your site in structured, unambiguous terms.

### What schema markup actually does

Without schema: AI reads your homepage and tries to figure out what your business is, where it is, what it does, and whether it's credible. It's guessing based on text patterns.

With schema: AI reads a machine-perfect declaration of exactly what you are, precisely formatted in a standard it was trained to trust.

Schema markup is written in JSON-LD format and placed in the `<head>` of your HTML. Here are the schemas that matter most for AI visibility:

### Schema 1: LocalBusiness (or subtype)

This is the most important schema for local and regional businesses.

```json
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "LocalBusiness",
  "name": "[Your Exact Business Name]",
  "image": "https://yourdomain.com/logo.jpg",
  "@id": "https://yourdomain.com",
  "url": "https://yourdomain.com",
  "telephone": "+1-555-555-5555",
  "address": {
    "@type": "PostalAddress",
    "streetAddress": "123 Main Street",
    "addressLocality": "Austin",
    "addressRegion": "TX",
    "postalCode": "78701",
    "addressCountry": "US"
  },
  "geo": {
    "@type": "GeoCoordinates",
    "latitude": 30.2672,
    "longitude": -97.7431
  },
  "openingHoursSpecification": [
    {
      "@type": "OpeningHoursSpecification",
      "dayOfWeek": ["Monday", "Tuesday", "Wednesday", "Thursday", "Friday"],
      "opens": "09:00",
      "closes": "17:00"
    }
  ],
  "sameAs": [
    "https://www.facebook.com/yourbusiness",
    "https://www.yelp.com/biz/yourbusiness",
    "https://www.linkedin.com/company/yourbusiness"
  ],
  "description": "[Your business description — 100 to 200 words. Same as or similar to your GBP description.]",
  "priceRange": "$$",
  "aggregateRating": {
    "@type": "AggregateRating",
    "ratingValue": "4.8",
    "reviewCount": "127"
  }
}
```

**Important:** Replace `LocalBusiness` with your specific business type if one exists. Google has hundreds of subtypes including: `Dentist`, `Attorney`, `AccountingService`, `Plumber`, `Restaurant`, `RealEstateAgent`, `FinancialService`, `ITConsulting`, and more. Using the specific type dramatically improves AI recognition. Search "schema.org [your business type]" to find yours.

### Schema 2: FAQPage

This is the fastest way to get AI to quote your content verbatim. AI models love FAQ content — it's pre-formatted as questions and answers, which is exactly how people use AI assistants.

Create a FAQ page or section on your site with 8–15 common questions about your business, and mark it up:

```json
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "FAQPage",
  "mainEntity": [
    {
      "@type": "Question",
      "name": "What services does [Business Name] offer?",
      "acceptedAnswer": {
        "@type": "Answer",
        "text": "[Business Name] offers [list of services]. We serve [location/area] and have been in business since [year]."
      }
    },
    {
      "@type": "Question",
      "name": "Where is [Business Name] located?",
      "acceptedAnswer": {
        "@type": "Answer",
        "text": "[Business Name] is located at [address] in [city, state]. We also serve [surrounding areas]."
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

**The right FAQ questions to write:**
Think about the exact questions a customer would ask an AI assistant about a business like yours. Examples:
- "What is the best [your business type] in [your city]?"
- "How much does [your service] cost?"
- "Is [Business Name] licensed and insured?"
- "How long has [Business Name] been in business?"
- "What do customers say about [Business Name]?"

Write your answers as complete, standalone sentences that would sound natural if an AI read them aloud as a recommendation.

### Schema 3: Organization (for service businesses, SaaS, and non-local businesses)

```json
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "Organization",
  "name": "[Business Name]",
  "url": "https://yourdomain.com",
  "logo": "https://yourdomain.com/logo.png",
  "description": "[200-word description of what you do, who you help, and what makes you credible]",
  "foundingDate": "[Year]",
  "founder": {
    "@type": "Person",
    "name": "[Founder Name]"
  },
  "numberOfEmployees": {
    "@type": "QuantitativeValue",
    "value": "[Number]"
  },
  "sameAs": [
    "[All your social media and directory profile URLs]"
  ]
}
```

### How to add schema to your website

**WordPress:** Use the free Rank Math or Yoast SEO plugin. Both have schema builders that don't require any coding.

**Squarespace / Wix:** Add a Code Block (Squarespace) or Custom HTML element (Wix) in your page header and paste the JSON-LD directly.

**Shopify:** Add schema via the `theme.liquid` file in `<head>`, or use an app like "JSON-LD for SEO."

**Custom site:** Paste the JSON-LD in a `<script type="application/ld+json">` tag in your `<head>` section.

**Validate your schema:** After adding, use [validator.schema.org](https://validator.schema.org) to confirm there are no errors.

## Part 4: Directory Citations — Building Your Credibility Footprint

AI models learn about businesses from directories. A business that appears on 25 major directories with consistent, detailed information is dramatically more credible to AI than one that only has a website.

### The 15 directories that matter most for AI visibility

Submit to all 15. In order of priority:

1. **Google Business Profile** (done in Foundation 1)
2. **Bing Places for Business** — [bingplaces.com](https://bingplaces.com) — powers Microsoft Copilot directly
3. **Apple Business Connect** — [register.apple.com/business](https://register.apple.com/business) — powers Apple Maps and Siri
4. **Yelp** — [biz.yelp.com](https://biz.yelp.com) — one of the highest-weighted review sources for AI
5. **BBB (Better Business Bureau)** — [bbb.org/start](https://bbb.org/start) — credibility signal, especially for Claude
6. **Facebook Business Page** — [facebook.com/pages/create](https://facebook.com/pages/create)
7. **LinkedIn Company Page** — [linkedin.com/company/setup/new](https://linkedin.com/company/setup/new)
8. **Foursquare** — [foursquare.com/add-place](https://foursquare.com/add-place) — feeds dozens of other platforms
9. **Yellow Pages** — [yellowpages.com](https://yellowpages.com)
10. **Superpages** — [superpages.com](https://superpages.com)
11. **Manta** — [manta.com](https://manta.com)
12. **Hotfrog** — [hotfrog.com](https://hotfrog.com)
13. **Citysearch** — [citysearch.com](https://citysearch.com)
14. **Chamber of Commerce** — Your local chamber's website directory
15. **Industry-specific directory** — Find the top directory specific to your industry (e.g., Avvo for lawyers, Healthgrades for healthcare, Houzz for contractors, Clutch for agencies)

### What to write in every directory listing

Use the same business description across all directories — but it must be genuinely descriptive, not generic. Template:

With [X years] of experience and [X reviews/clients], we [unique value proposition]. [Business Name] is [certifications/licenses/associations]. Contact us at [phone] or visit [website].*

Fill in every field available on every directory. The more complete your profile, the more data points AI has to pull from.

## Part 5: Content That AI Quotes

AI assistants don't just know about businesses from directories. They actively pull from website content, blog posts, and FAQ pages when forming recommendations. This section is about creating content AI wants to quote.

### The content types AI quotes most

**FAQ pages and FAQ schema** (covered in Part 3) — AI loves Q&A format because it mirrors how users ask questions.

**Comparison content** — "[Your Service] vs. [Alternative]: Which is Right for You?" — AI frequently quotes comparison content when users ask "should I use X or Y?"

**Process explanation content** — "How [Your Process] Works: A Step-by-Step Guide" — AI quotes this when users ask "how does [service] work?"

**Credential and authority content** — Pages that list your certifications, awards, years in business, and credentials. AI uses these to justify recommendations: "[Business Name] has been a licensed [profession] for 15 years and holds [certification]..."

**Location-specific pages** — If you serve multiple cities or neighborhoods, a dedicated page for each one dramatically improves AI visibility for location-specific queries.

### The "Quotable Paragraph" technique

Every page on your website should have at least one paragraph that reads like something an AI assistant would quote verbatim when recommending your business. It should:
- Start with your business name
- State what you do and who you serve
- Include a credibility signal (years, certifications, number of clients)
- Be 3–5 complete, well-written sentences
- Sound natural when read aloud

Example:
*"Smith Plumbing has served the Austin area since 2008, specializing in residential and commercial plumbing repair, installation, and emergency services. Licensed and insured in Texas, we've completed over 4,000 service calls for homeowners and property managers across Travis and Williamson counties. Our team of six licensed plumbers is available 24/7 for emergencies and holds a 4.9-star average across 312 Google reviews."*

If ChatGPT read that paragraph, it would have everything it needs to recommend you.

### How often to publish content

For AI visibility purposes, you need at minimum:
- 1 new piece of content per month
- Content should answer specific questions your ideal customer asks
- Each piece should include your business name, location, and service type in the first paragraph
- Each piece should have FAQPage schema with 3–5 Q&As at the bottom

You don't need to write 2,000-word essays. A well-structured 600-word piece that directly answers one specific question and includes proper schema is worth more for AI visibility than a 3,000-word rambling post.

## Part 6: Getting Press and Third-Party Mentions

The most powerful signal for AI credibility — especially for Claude and ChatGPT — is being mentioned in sources that AI considers authoritative. This includes local news, industry publications, and high-authority blogs.

### Why press matters so much

Think of it this way: if your business is only described on your own website and your own directory listings, AI is reading your own marketing. But if a local news outlet wrote about you, an industry blog mentioned you, and you're referenced in a community magazine — now AI is reading *other people's* assessments of you. That's a categorically different trust signal.

### How to get press mentions without a PR agency

**1. Local newspaper / news site**
Most local newspapers are desperate for business stories. Email the business editor directly:

*Subject: Local business story idea — [your angle]*

*Hi [Name],*

*I'm [your name], owner of [Business Name] in [city]. We recently [did something newsworthy: hired X employees, launched a new service, hit a milestone, partnered with a community organization]. I think there might be a story here for your readers.*

*Happy to provide quotes, photos, and data. Would a brief call work?*

*[Your name, phone, email]*

**2. HARO / Connectively (Help a Reporter Out)**
Sign up at [connectively.us](https://connectively.us). Journalists post daily requests for expert sources. When a request matches your expertise, respond with a concise, quotable answer. Even one mention in a national publication is worth months of other AI visibility work.

**3. Industry association press**
Every major industry association has a blog, newsletter, or member spotlight. Pitch them a case study or how-to piece. These carry high credibility with AI because they're authoritative, niche-specific sources.

**4. Podcast guest appearances**
Podcasts often have transcripts, show notes, and articles associated with episodes. A guest appearance on a podcast with a transcript online creates a high-quality mention with your name, expertise, and business mentioned in context.

**5. Community sponsorships and events**
Sponsoring a local event or community organization often gets you mentioned on their website with a link. These citations — especially from .org, .edu, or local government websites — are extremely high-value for AI.

### The minimum press goal
3 editorial mentions per year from sources you don't control. That's one every four months. It's achievable for any business that puts in modest effort.

## Part 7: Wikidata — AI's Most Trusted Directory

Wikidata is the structured data layer behind Wikipedia. It's the closest thing to an official "AI directory" that exists. When AI assistants need a reliable, structured reference for a business, Wikidata is one of the first places they look.

Creating a Wikidata entry for your business is free, public, and permanent. It's also something almost no small business has done.

### Should you create a Wikidata entry?

Wikidata is best suited for:
- Businesses that have been around for more than 2 years
- Businesses with at least some third-party references (news articles, directory listings, etc.)
- Businesses or founders who have some level of public profile

If you're a brand new business with no press mentions, hold off until you have some third-party citations to reference.

### How to create your Wikidata entry

1. Go to [wikidata.org](https://wikidata.org) and create a free account
2. Click "Create a new item"
3. Set the label to your exact business name
4. Add a description: "[type of business] in [city, state]"
5. Add the following statements (properties):
   - **instance of** (P31): use "business" or the specific type (e.g., "law firm", "restaurant")
   - **country** (P17): United States of America
   - **located in** (P131): your city/state
   - **official website** (P856): your website URL
   - **inception** (P571): your founding year
   - **founded by** (P112): create or link to an entry for the founder
6. Under "References," add your website and any news articles that mention you

Once your entry exists and has at least 3–4 statements with references, it becomes a data point AI models actively use.

## Part 8: Social Proof Amplification

Reviews are cited by AI assistants more than almost any other signal. When someone asks "is [Business Name] any good?", AI looks for review data first.

### The review strategy that actually works

**Volume beats perfection.** A business with 200 reviews averaging 4.6 stars will be recommended over a business with 12 reviews averaging 5.0. AI models see high review counts as a signal of established, trusted businesses.

**Respond to every review.** Every response you write gets indexed. Use your business name, mention the service, and thank the reviewer naturally. Don't use a template — AI can tell.

**Get reviews on multiple platforms.** Google reviews are the most important, but also build your presence on:
- Yelp (very heavily weighted, especially for Perplexity)
- Facebook
- Your industry-specific platform (TripAdvisor, Healthgrades, Avvo, etc.)
- Trustpilot (for online businesses)
- G2 or Capterra (for SaaS/software)

**The review ask template** (for email):

*Subject: A quick favor*

*Hi [Name],*

*Thank you again for choosing [Business Name]. It was great working with you.*

*If you have 2 minutes, we'd really appreciate a Google review — it helps other [type of customer] find us and know what to expect.*

*[Direct link to your Google review page]*

*Thank you,*
*[Your name]*

## Part 9: Testing Your AI Visibility

All of this work is pointless if you can't measure whether it's working. Here's how to test your AI visibility systematically.

### The manual testing protocol

Once a month, run these exact tests in all four assistants. Copy the responses into a document. Date them. Track your progress.

**Test 1 — Direct name recognition:**
"What can you tell me about [Business Name] in [City, State]?"

**Test 2 — Category recommendation:**
"What are the best [your business type] in [your city]?"

**Test 3 — Problem-based query:**
"Who should I hire for [your main service] in [your city]?"

**Test 4 — Comparison query:**
"What are the most reputable [your business type] in [your area] with good reviews?"

**Test 5 — Credential query:**
"Which [your business type] in [your city] have been around the longest?"

### What good looks like

- **Month 1 (baseline):** Likely not mentioned or mentioned with limited/uncertain information
- **Month 2–3:** Mentioned in direct name queries; starting to appear in category recommendations on some assistants
- **Month 4–6:** Consistently mentioned across 3+ assistants; appearing in recommendation lists without being asked directly about you

### What to do if you're not making progress

If after 60 days of implementing this playbook you're still not being mentioned:

1. Check schema validation — errors in schema are invisible to users but break AI crawling
2. Audit NAP consistency again — a single inconsistency on a major platform can suppress all other signals
3. Confirm your site is crawlable — check robots.txt and use Google Search Console to see if pages are indexed
4. Look for a name conflict — is there a similarly named business in your area? You may need to make your schema and content more specific
5. Accelerate press mentions — for businesses with very low web footprint, third-party mentions are the fastest lever

## Part 10: Advanced Tactics for Getting the Edge

Once the foundations are solid, these advanced tactics can push you from "sometimes mentioned" to "consistently recommended."

### Create your own press page

A `/press` or `/in-the-news` page on your website that aggregates every mention, award, and citation your business has received. With proper schema, AI treats this page as a curated credibility summary.

### Build topical authority with a content hub

Pick the 3–5 questions your ideal customers ask most often. Write a comprehensive, well-structured answer for each one. Link them together. Over time, AI models begin to recognize your website as an authoritative source on those topics — and start recommending you for queries that aren't even direct business searches.

### Use your email signature

Add a link to your GBP review page and your Yelp page in your email signature. You send dozens to hundreds of emails per week. Every satisfied recipient who clicks and leaves a review compounds your visibility.

### Create a "Best of [City]" or "Guide to [Service]" resource
