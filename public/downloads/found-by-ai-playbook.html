Part 1 — How AI Decides Who to Mention

It’s not a ranking algorithm

Google ranks pages. AI assistants generate answers. That distinction changes everything. Google asks: which page best matches this query? ChatGPT asks: what is the most accurate, helpful answer I can construct? Your business gets mentioned if the AI has enough reliable information about you to include you confidently.

You cannot pay to appear. You cannot keyword-stuff your way in. You earn inclusion by being a clearly defined, well-documented, credible entity across the web.

The three questions every AI asks about your business

1

Does this business clearly exist?

Entity clarity. Does the AI have enough consistent, structured information to know you’re a real, operating business — not a duplicate, not defunct, not ambiguous?

2

Is this business credible?

Authority signals. Is your business mentioned in trustworthy sources — directories, reviews, press, industry listings?

3

Is this business relevant to this query?

Attribute matching. Does what the AI knows about you match what the user is asking — right category, location, specialty, service type?

Everything in this playbook is designed to give AI a confident yes to all three.

How the four major AIs behave differently

Key insight: Perplexity updates fast (days to weeks). ChatGPT and Claude update slowly (weeks to months). Gemini is in between. Build the foundation once — it compounds across all four.

Part 2 — The Foundation (Do These First)

Step 1: Claim and complete your Google Business Profile

Why: Google’s Knowledge Graph is a primary data source for all four AI assistants. A complete, verified GBP is the highest single-leverage action most businesses can take.

Go to: business.google.com — claim and verify your listing.

Fill every field. No exceptions:

  • Business name — exactly as it legally appears, no keyword stuffing
  • Primary category — be specific ("Family Dentist," not "Dentist")
  • Secondary categories — add 2–3 relevant ones
  • Address — full address including suite; match it exactly everywhere else
  • Phone — local number preferred over 800 number
  • Website — your actual homepage URL
  • Hours — accurate, including holiday hours
  • Description — use the formula below (750 characters)
  • Services / Products — fill this out completely
  • Photos — minimum 10: exterior, interior, team, work examples, logo

GBP description formula:

[Business Name] is a [type] in [City, State] specializing in [service 1], [service 2], and [service 3]. We serve [customer type] in [service area]. [One sentence on what makes you different — years of experience, awards, specialty.]

Example: Lakeview Dental is a family dental practice in Chicago, Illinois specializing in cosmetic dentistry, Invisalign, and emergency dental care. We serve families and adults throughout Lincoln Park and Lakeview. Dr. Sarah Chen has 18 years of experience and is a certified Invisalign provider.

Step 2: Add schema markup to your website

Why: Schema markup is code that tells AI crawlers exactly who you are, what you do, and where you are — in a language machines read fluently. Without it, AI infers from unstructured text and often gets it wrong.

You need three schemas. Copy, fill in, and add each one inside a <script type="application/ld+json"> tag in the <head> of the relevant page.

Schema A — LocalBusiness (Homepage)

{{ "@context": "https://schema.org", "@type": "LocalBusiness", "name": "YOUR BUSINESS NAME", "description": "YOUR DESCRIPTION (match your GBP exactly)", "url": "https://yourwebsite.com", "telephone": "+1-XXX-XXX-XXXX", "email": "your@email.com", "address": {{ "@type": "PostalAddress", "streetAddress": "123 Main Street", "addressLocality": "Your City", "addressRegion": "XX", "postalCode": "00000", "addressCountry": "US" }}, "openingHoursSpecification": [{{ "@type": "OpeningHoursSpecification", "dayOfWeek": ["Monday","Tuesday","Wednesday","Thursday","Friday"], "opens": "09:00", "closes": "17:00" }}], "priceRange": "$$", "areaServed": "Your City, Your State", "sameAs": [ "https://www.facebook.com/yourbusiness", "https://www.yelp.com/biz/your-listing", "https://g.page/your-listing" ] }}

Tip: Replace “LocalBusiness” with the most specific type that fits: Restaurant, LegalService, MedicalBusiness, HomeAndConstructionBusiness, HealthAndBeautyBusiness, AutomotiveBusiness. Full list at schema.org/LocalBusiness.

Schema B — Organization (About page)

{{ "@context": "https://schema.org", "@type": "Organization", "name": "YOUR BUSINESS NAME", "url": "https://yourwebsite.com", "logo": "https://yourwebsite.com/logo.png", "foundingDate": "YYYY", "description": "YOUR DESCRIPTION", "founder": {{ "@type": "Person", "name": "FOUNDER NAME" }}, "contactPoint": {{ "@type": "ContactPoint", "telephone": "+1-XXX-XXX-XXXX", "contactType": "customer service" }}, "sameAs": [ "https://www.linkedin.com/company/yourbusiness", "https://twitter.com/yourbusiness" ] }}

Schema C — FAQPage (FAQ or About page)

{{ "@context": "https://schema.org", "@type": "FAQPage", "mainEntity": [ {{ "@type": "Question", "name": "What services does [Business] offer?", "acceptedAnswer": {{ "@type": "Answer", "text": "Full answer here." }} }}, {{ "@type": "Question", "name": "Where is [Business] located?", "acceptedAnswer": {{ "@type": "Answer", "text": "Full address and service area here." }} }}, {{ "@type": "Question", "name": "How long has [Business] been operating?", "acceptedAnswer": {{ "@type": "Answer", "text": "Founded in [year], [Business] has [X] years of experience." }} }} ] }}

Validate it: After adding any schema, go to search.google.com/test/rich-results and paste your URL. It should show zero critical errors before you move on.

Step 3: Build your citation footprint

Why: AI models verify a business is real by finding consistent mentions across multiple independent platforms. This is your “citation footprint.” The rule: your Name, Address, and Phone (NAP) must appear identically on every platform. Even small variations (St. vs Street, Suite vs Ste.) dilute the signal.

Define your NAP source of truth right now and never deviate from it:

Business Name: [Exactly as it legally appears] Address: [Street Number] [Street Name], [Suite if any], [City], [ST] [ZIP] Phone: (XXX) XXX-XXXX Website: https://yourwebsite.com

Part 3 — The Authority Layer

Step 4: Rewrite your About page so AI can quote it

Why: Your About page is dense with facts about who you are. AI systems crawl it specifically to answer questions like “what does [Business] do?” and “who is behind [Business]?” Most About pages are vague and written for tone, not facts. Fix yours.

The AI-optimized About page formula:

1

The What (2–3 sentences)

State exactly what you do, for whom, and where. Use the same description as your GBP. No metaphors. No mission statement fluff.

2

The Credentials (bullet list)

Years in business, number of customers served, licenses, certifications, awards, notable clients, specializations. Anything that signals credibility to a stranger.

3

The Facts (dates and numbers)

Founded: [year]. Location: [full address]. Service area: [list cities/regions]. Team size: [X]. Phone: [number]. Email: [address]. The more specific the facts, the more quotable you are.

4

The Human (optional but valuable)

A short paragraph about the founder. Name, background, why they started the business. AI models use this to humanize and contextualize mentions.

Step 5: Create a /facts page

Why: AI models love Wikipedia. They’re trained on it. You probably don’t have a Wikipedia page — but you can create the equivalent on your own site. A /facts page (yourwebsite.com/facts) is a structured, factual reference document about your business that AI can pull from directly.

What to include:

  • Full business name (legal entity name)
  • Founded: [month, year], by [founder name]
  • Headquarters / location: full address
  • Service area: list every city, region, or neighborhood you serve
  • Primary services: bullet list, one line each, no marketing language
  • Specializations: what you’re known for above and beyond the basics
  • Team: key staff names and roles
  • Licenses & certifications: license numbers, issuing body, state
  • Awards & recognition: year, award name, issuing organization
  • Notable clients or projects: if permissible
  • Media mentions: publication name, date, brief description, link
  • Social profiles: links to every active platform
  • Contact: phone, email, website, hours

Format tip: Use plain HTML with a clear heading hierarchy (H1 for the page title, H2 for each section, plain paragraphs and bullets). No JavaScript. No fancy layouts. You want a page that loads fast and is pure, crawlable text.

Step 6: Get your first editorial mention

Why: AI models weight businesses that are mentioned by third-party authoritative sources — not just self-reported data. Even one strong editorial mention dramatically improves AI credibility signals. This is the hardest step, but it’s also the highest leverage once done.

The fastest paths to an editorial mention:

  • Local press: Email your city’s local paper or news site with a genuine news angle — new opening, milestone, community initiative, unique service. Journalists on local beats are often looking for filler stories. One email can land a mention.
  • Chamber of commerce: Join your local chamber. Member spotlights, newsletters, and event features provide indexed mentions that AI models see as credible third-party endorsements.
  • Industry association listings: Most trade associations maintain member directories. These are clean, authoritative, and highly weighted by AI for category-specific queries.
  • HARO / Connectively: Sign up at connectively.us. Journalists post requests for expert sources daily. Respond fast to relevant queries. A single quoted mention in an online article is worth dozens of directory listings for AI trust.
  • Guest posts: Offer to write a short practical post for a local or industry blog. Even modest Domain Authority sites provide valid third-party entity confirmation.

Step 7: Run a review campaign

Why: Reviews are a credibility signal AI models use explicitly. They signal: this business is real, active, and used by actual humans. Volume, recency, and platform spread all matter.

The fastest review acquisition process:

  1. Identify your last 20–30 satisfied customers (check email, CRM, invoices)
  2. Send a personal 2-sentence text or email: “Hey [Name], really appreciate your business. If you have 2 minutes, a Google review would mean a lot to me — here’s the direct link: [your GBP review link].”
  3. Follow up once, 5 days later, for anyone who didn’t respond
  4. Add a “Leave us a review” card / QR code to receipts, invoices, or email signatures going forward

Target: 15+ Google reviews, 5+ Yelp reviews, 3+ Facebook reviews as a baseline. Once achieved, reviews from real customers compound automatically if you keep asking.

Part 4 — Technical & Tracking

Step 8: Set up Google Search Console

GSC tells Google your site exists, is active, and what pages it contains. Submit your sitemap so your pages are indexed — unindexed pages are invisible to AI systems that use Google’s index.

  1. Go to search.google.com/search-console
  2. Add your domain property and verify ownership (HTML tag in <head> is easiest)
  3. Go to Sitemaps → enter your sitemap URL (usually yoursite.com/sitemap.xml) → Submit
  4. Check monthly for crawl errors and fix any that appear

If you don’t have a sitemap: WordPress (Yoast creates one), Squarespace/Wix (auto at yourdomain.com/sitemap.xml), custom site (generate free at xml-sitemaps.com).

Step 9: Set up Bing Webmaster Tools

Microsoft Copilot (one of the fastest-growing AI assistants) pulls heavily from Bing’s index. Takes 5 minutes to set up and is almost universally skipped by small businesses — which means it’s a fast win.

  1. Go to bing.com/webmasters and sign in with a Microsoft account
  2. Add your site, import from Google Search Console (easiest method)
  3. Submit your sitemap

Step 10: Track your AI visibility

You can’t improve what you don’t measure. Use the prompt pack (PDF 3 in your bundle) to run a quarterly AI audit. The process:

  1. Run all 10 prompts once every 90 days
  2. Screenshot every response
  3. Note: are you mentioned? Is the information accurate? Are competitors mentioned instead?
  4. Identify the biggest gap and prioritize it for the next 30 days

Over 3–4 quarters you’ll see a clear trend. Most businesses that implement this playbook move from “not mentioned” to “occasionally mentioned” in 30–60 days, and to “consistently recommended” within 90–120 days.

Part 5 — Fixing AI Hallucinations

Sometimes AI assistants get your business wrong. Wrong hours. Wrong address. Wrong services. Wrong founding year. This happens when the AI’s available data is incomplete or contradictory and it fills gaps with educated guesses.

How to find the problem

Ask each AI directly: “Tell me everything you know about [Business Name] in [City].” and “What are the hours, address, and services of [Business Name]?” Screenshot everything. Any wrong fact is a data conflict you need to resolve.

How to fix it

You cannot directly edit AI responses — but you can flood the web with accurate, authoritative signals that override bad data over time. For every wrong fact you find:

  1. Correct it on your GBP immediately
  2. Correct it on every directory that has the wrong information
  3. Update your schema markup to reflect the accurate information
  4. Add a clear statement of the correct fact to your /facts page
  5. If the error is significant (wrong city, wrong category), pursue a press mention that states the accurate information — editorial citations carry the most weight in overriding hallucinations

Timeline: Perplexity updates in days to weeks. ChatGPT and Claude update in weeks to months. Be patient and consistent. The accurate signal always wins if it’s louder than the inaccurate one.
